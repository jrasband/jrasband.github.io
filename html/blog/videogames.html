Video Games Aren't Worth It

Introduction

I used to love playing video games. When I a young teenager, it was my favorite way to spend my time. A decade later, I understand more clearly the costs and benefits associated with playing video games. My hope is that my thoughts might help others make better, more informed decisions about how they spend their time.

Video Games Are Fun

People play video games because they are fun. Tastes may vary, but for most people there is a genre of video game that they find entertaining. Entertainment (in moderation) can be very beneficial, but costs must also be considered. Plenty of activities may be fun but have costs associated with them, for example, gambling, shopping, or eating dessert. Although something is fun to do, it's important to determine how much time (and money) an activity deserves in your life.

Video games may be fun, but they don't have a monopoly on fun. With a little bit of creativity and adventurousness, one can find dozens of new hobbies, pastimes, and diversions that can and will be just as much fun as video games. Importantly, fun alternatives to video games (such as cooking or sports) can yield substantial real-world benefits in the long run.

Video Games Are Social

Perhaps the next-most popular reason for playing video games is because it's a social activity that one does with friends. This is an argument that I support, with a caveat. In-person social gaming is vastly superior to remote social gaming. The social benefits drop to be almost negligible when there isn't any real-time face-to-face interaction. Interpersonal human socialization depends heavily on facial expressions, body language, and eye movements. Even though you might be able to catch up with your buddies via a purely online game, there just isn't any substitute for hanging out together in real life. So, if you're playing video games in-person with friends or family for a few (this means two to three) hours a week, I'll call this argument in your favor. If you aren't actually seeing your friends face to face, I don't think the marginal benefit from superficial internet interaction is going to outweigh the other costs (of money, health, and time) you incur by playing video games.

Do Video Games "Teach Skills?"

Some claim that video games build problem-solving skills, teach strategy, history, and improve hand-eye coordination. To a certain extent, each of these are true, but I don't accept that these are the reasons why people play video games. There are activities that are more efficient for improving hand-eye coordination and learning history, such as playing sports or reading a history book. Video games are at best an imperfect simulation about the real world. In every case, the real world is a better way to learn about the real world. To those who make this argument: search your heart, and you'll probably find that the real reason you play video games is probably a combination of "video games are fun" and "video games are social."

Video Games Are a Slow Bleed

The costs of video games are hard to notice because they're a slow bleed. One day of video games may not cost very much, but a lifetime habit of playing video games incurs huge costs in time, money, and health.

Video Games Cost Money

It isn't always true that video games cost money just to play (e.g. free-to-play mobile games), but serious "gamers" often end up spending lots of money on consoles, monitors, custom desktop builds, and accessories. After hardware costs, there are also the costs of buying the games themselves, in-game purchases, and potentially subscription services related to gaming. Assume that a $2K desktop and a $1.5K monitor last for 5 years and you spend only $500 on games over that 5-year period. You could be even more frugal than that, but this is a good way to estimate the costs. In total, $4K of video gaming expenses over 5 years comes out to a little over $2 per day. That may not sound like much, but it's equivalent in cost to buying a pack of cigarettes every three days, or a cup of coffee every other day. (Yes, I did just choose habits with negative connotations to make my point that I think video gaming is a costly, somewhat additctive behavior, but at least I'm conscious of the biased comparison I made and I'm letting you know about it. If I were convinced that video games had long-term health benefits, then maybe I'd compare the cost of video gaming to the cost of buying fresh produce or the cost of buying exercise equipment. I'll get to the health costs of video games later.)

The estimate given above is for low-end, casual gaming. If someone is serious about gaming, they might replace a $5K desktop build every 3 years, have multiple high-end monitors, and spend multiple hundreds of dollars a year on new games, making the daily cost of their gaming habit as much or more than the cost of a pack of cigarettes a day (about $6). Again, I don't think that most people consciously decide that they are going to spend a significant portion of their income on video games. Rather, it's a habit that grows and grows into a steady bleed of money, time, and health.

Video Games Cost Time

You may already be familiar with the idea of "opportunity cost." If not, here's a simple explanation: opportunity cost is the next-best thing, the thing that you give up when you spend your time or money. For example, the opportunity cost of buying a BMW might be buying a Mercedes (if those were the only two options). If I buy the BMW, I give up the opportunity to buy the Mercedes, and vice versa. In real life, there are more than just two options, and it's much harder to compare the value of your options.

The real opportunity cost of video games is that the benefits of playing video games don't compound over time. I would argue that many of the activities you give up for gaming experience compounded returns on investment. Real-life time with family and friends generates increasing return on investment as relationships deepen and become more meaningful. A hobby or useful skill such as cooking can likewise develop into a rewarding mastery. (I highly recommend getting good at cooking --- it is one of the easiest ways that I know of to improve your overall quality of life.) The skills developed in video games are not life-long, and they don't compound. It's unlikely that you'll be playing the same video game ten years from now, but you will most likely still have to cook for yourself. There will still be valuable relationships in your life that are worthy of your time. Choose activities that experience compounding returns on investment, not activities that offer no reward beyond instant (and temporary) pleasure.

Video Games Cost Health

Although not all who play video games are unhealthy, it should be obvious that a video game habit will exacerbate the already sedentary modern lifestyle. Especially in the United States, under-exercising is a health crisis with very real human costs. Physical exercise can be as fun as playing video games. It takes more effort, but that's part of what makes it more rewarding. Solo sports, community team sports, and going for walks are so much better than doing nothing at all. Finding something active that you enjoy doing (or that you think you /will/ enjoy) will lead to lifelong benefits in physical and mental health.

The mental costs of playing video games may be greater than the physical costs. Those who play video games are much more likely to stay up late and lose sleep. Competitive gaming can be stressful and fellow players can be toxic. Although not all who play video games incur these costs, they are common enough to deserve mention.

Video Games Are a Substitute

I said previously that the main reason people play video games is because they are fun. Why are video games so much fun? Video games simluate things in the real world that are fun or fulfilling. In video games, one can effortlessly explore, make friends, conquer enemies, and save the world. All it takes is pushing the right combination of buttons at the right times. Saving the world is much harder in real life. Just like food substitutes are never as satisfying as the real thing (for example, margarine in place of butter) the sense of accomplishment that video games provide is a substitute that will never be as satisfying as the feelings that come from achievement in the real world. For me, this realization struck me like lightning. In an instant, I could see that the reason I found video games fun or fulfilling was because I wasn't accomplishing very much in real life. Instead of building real-world skills or relationships, I was spending time learning how to push buttons in the right order for a sense of achievement that left as soon as I returned to reality. The principle of avoiding substitutes is a versitile principle which has served me very well.

Conclusion

After examining the reasons why I played video games and stacking up the long-term costs, I determined that video games didn't deserve a large part of my life. That rebalancing freed up more time in my life for personal relationships, focusing on my physical and mental health, building new skills, and real-world achievement that satisfied the needs that led me to playing video games in the first place.
